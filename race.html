Race

How did race structure the experience of incarceration at Attica and the analysis of power that emerged from within?

The Racial Architecture of Attica

The demographics of Attica in 1971 were not accidental. A facility holding over 2,200 people—the majority Black and Puerto Rican—was staffed by a virtually all-white officer corps and administered by an entirely white leadership. This racial composition mirrored the broader structure of American criminal punishment, in which communities of color were disproportionately policed, prosecuted, sentenced, and confined, while the apparatus of control remained in white hands.

For the incarcerated people at Attica, this racial structure was not simply a background condition. It was the central fact of their confinement. The daily humiliations, the restrictions on cultural and religious practice, the denial of adequate food and medical care, and the routine physical abuse they experienced were understood through the lens of race. The prison was not a racially neutral institution that happened to hold a disproportionate number of Black and Brown people. It was, in their analysis, a racial institution—a mechanism for the control and containment of communities that the American state deemed threatening.

Race and Solidarity

One of the most significant intellectual achievements of the Attica organizing was the construction of interracial solidarity within the prison. The Attica Liberation Faction and the broader prisoner movement brought together Black, Puerto Rican, and white prisoners around a shared analysis of the prison as a system of oppression. This solidarity was not easy or automatic. It required sustained political education and the development of a framework that could account for the specific racial dynamics of incarceration while also identifying common ground.

The 28 demands presented during the uprising reflected this interracial coalition. They addressed conditions that affected all prisoners—medical care, food, censorship, legal rights—while also foregrounding specifically racialized grievances, such as the demand for an end to racist treatment by guards and for access to culturally relevant programming. The capacity to build solidarity across racial lines within an institution designed to divide and control is itself a form of knowledge production: it required an analysis of power sophisticated enough to distinguish between the racial hierarchies that existed among prisoners and the structural racism that oppressed all of them.

Race and the State Response

The racial dimensions of the state's response to the uprising are difficult to overstate. Governor Rockefeller's refusal to negotiate personally, the decision to launch an armed assault, and the near-indiscriminate nature of the shooting all must be understood in the context of a political moment in which Black collective action was treated as an existential threat to state authority.

The misinformation campaign that followed the assault—the false claim that prisoners had slit hostages' throats—drew on deeply racialized tropes of Black savagery. The lie was believed because it confirmed existing racial assumptions about Black violence, and it served a political function: it retroactively justified the state's lethal response and insulated decision-makers from accountability. When Dr. Edland's autopsies revealed the truth, the correction received far less attention than the original misinformation.

The false narrative of throat-slitting was not simply an error. It was a racial narrative—a story that could only be told, and only be believed, because of centuries of anti-Black mythology that cast Black people as inherently savage. — Analytical reflection

Race and Accountability

The asymmetry of legal accountability after Attica was itself a racial phenomenon. Sixty-two incarcerated individuals—overwhelmingly people of color—faced 1,289 criminal counts. One state trooper was indicted. The investigative failures that prevented prosecution of law-enforcement officers were attributed to "flagrant deficiencies" in evidence collection by the same state police force that had carried out the assault.

This pattern—aggressive prosecution of the confined and near-total impunity for the state—reflects what scholars have described as the racial logic of the carceral system. The prison does not simply confine racialized bodies; it produces a framework in which the violence done to those bodies is rendered invisible, justified, or simply unimportant. The decades-long sealing of the Meyer Report's most critical volumes is one expression of this logic: the state's violence against Black and Brown prisoners was not merely unpunished but actively concealed.

Racial Knowledge from Confinement

The incarcerated people of Attica produced a racial analysis of American society that anticipated many of the insights later developed by scholars of mass incarceration, racial capitalism, and the carceral state. They understood that the prison was not an aberration in American democracy but an expression of its foundational racial logic. They recognized that their confinement was connected to the confinement of their communities—through housing segregation, economic exclusion, police violence, and the systematic denial of political power.

This knowledge was produced not despite confinement but through it. The prison, by concentrating people who had been subjected to the full range of American racial violence, created the conditions for a collective analysis that might not have emerged in the same way outside the walls. The tragedy of Attica is that this knowledge was met not with engagement but with gunfire.

"The prison did not simply hold Black and Brown bodies. It was built for them. And when those bodies organized, the state answered with the only language it had left: force." — Analytical reflection
