Religion

How did religion function in relation to confinement—as resistance, critique, solidarity, or contradiction?

Religion Inside the Walls

At Attica, religion was never simply a matter of private devotion. It functioned as a site of collective organizing, a language of moral critique, a framework for asserting human dignity, and a source of tension with prison administrators who sought to control which forms of worship were permissible. Understanding the religious dimensions of the Attica uprising requires moving beyond the idea that religion "comforts" the incarcerated and instead asking how religious practice and thought became tools of resistance and intellectual production within a system designed to suppress both.

Islam and the Politics of Recognition

By 1971, the Nation of Islam and other Muslim movements had become significant forces within American prisons. At Attica, Muslim prisoners were among the most organized and politically articulate groups. Their demands included the right to practice their religion freely—access to ministers, dietary accommodations, and the ability to gather for prayer—demands that the overwhelmingly white, Christian prison administration treated with suspicion and hostility.

The struggle for religious recognition was not incidental to the larger political struggle. For Muslim prisoners, the refusal to acknowledge Islam was inseparable from the racial logic of incarceration itself. The prison's insistence on controlling religious practice reflected a broader project of cultural erasure: to confine not only bodies but identities, beliefs, and the capacity for self-definition.

Islam offered incarcerated people a counter-narrative to the dehumanization of the prison. It provided a framework for understanding their confinement as part of a larger structure of racial oppression, and it offered a vision of dignity and community that the prison sought to deny. In this sense, the demand for religious freedom was always also a demand for political recognition.

Religion as Resistance

The role of religion at Attica illustrates a broader pattern in the history of incarceration: religious practice becomes a vehicle for resistance precisely because it asserts the humanity of people the state has designated as less than human. When prisoners at Attica gathered for prayer, studied religious texts together, or demanded the right to observe dietary laws, they were performing acts of self-determination within a system built to eliminate self-determination.

This dynamic was not unique to Islam. Christian prisoners, particularly those influenced by Black liberation theology, also drew on religious language to articulate their critique of the prison. The manifesto presented during the uprising, while primarily a political document, carried moral and theological weight: its assertion that "we are men" echoed biblical and theological traditions of human dignity that challenged the dehumanizing logic of incarceration.

The demand for religious freedom inside the prison was never merely about worship. It was about asserting the right to an inner life that the state could not reach—a form of freedom that persisted even within the walls. — Analytical reflection

The Theological Stakes of State Violence

The state assault on September 13 carried theological as well as political significance. The willingness of the state to kill both prisoners and hostages in order to reassert control raised fundamental questions about the moral foundations of punishment. If the state is willing to sacrifice the lives of the very people it claims to be rescuing, what does that reveal about the true purpose of the prison?

For many incarcerated people and their communities, the violence at Attica confirmed what religious critique had long suggested: that the American penal system was not a system of justice but a system of sacrifice, in which certain bodies—overwhelmingly Black and Brown bodies—were offered up to maintain a social order built on racial hierarchy. This reading of incarceration as a form of ritualized violence connects the Attica uprising to deeper theological traditions that question the relationship between suffering, power, and redemption.

Religion and the Limits of Reform

After Attica, reforms included expanded access to religious programming and chaplaincy services. The New York Department of Corrections eventually recognized the right of Muslim prisoners to practice their faith. But these reforms raise their own questions: Does the institutional accommodation of religion neutralize its radical potential? When the prison permits prayer, does it absorb religion into the logic of management, transforming a tool of resistance into a mechanism of pacification?

This tension—between religion as resistance and religion as management—remains unresolved. It reflects a larger paradox of reform: the carceral state can adapt to demands for dignity without fundamentally altering the structures that produce indignity. The religious knowledge produced at Attica challenges us to ask whether genuine freedom of conscience is possible within an institution designed to control every dimension of human life.

"The prison tried to confine their bodies and silence their voices. But faith provided a language the walls could not contain." — Analytical reflection
