Religion

How did religion function in relation to confinement—as resistance, critique, solidarity, or contradiction?

Religion Inside the Walls

At Attica, religion was never simply a matter of private devotion. It functioned as a site of collective organizing, a language of moral critique, a framework for asserting human dignity, and a source of tension with prison administrators who sought to control which forms of worship were permissible. Understanding the religious dimensions of the Attica uprising requires moving beyond the idea that religion merely “comforts” the incarcerated and instead examining how religious practice and thought became tools of resistance within a system designed to suppress both.12

Islam and the Politics of Recognition

By 1971, Muslim prisoners had become one of the most organized groups inside Attica. Many were influenced by the Nation of Islam, which had expanded rapidly in American prisons during the 1950s and 1960s.13 Muslim prisoners demanded recognition of basic religious rights, including access to Muslim ministers, the ability to pray collectively, and accommodations for Islamic dietary restrictions.14

These concerns appeared directly in the prisoners’ manifesto. One demand called for a pork-free diet, since pork dominated the prison menu and violated Islamic dietary laws.14 Religious freedom therefore became intertwined with broader demands for dignity, fair treatment, and institutional recognition.

Prison officials often treated Muslim organizing with suspicion, viewing it as a potential security threat rather than as a legitimate exercise of religious liberty.13 For many Black prisoners, however, the teachings of the Nation of Islam offered a framework of discipline, racial pride, and collective identity within an institution designed to erase those qualities.

At the same time, Attica became a space of broader political discussion. Prisoners influenced by organizations such as the Black Panther Party and the Young Lords held meetings and discussions that concerned prison authorities.14 While these groups differed in ideology, they contributed to a climate of political awareness and collective organizing inside the prison.

Religion as Resistance

Religious practice at Attica often functioned as a form of resistance. Collective prayer, scriptural study, and adherence to religious dietary rules allowed prisoners to assert control over parts of their lives within an institution designed to regulate nearly every aspect of daily existence.

This dynamic can be understood through the framework of liberation theology. Liberation theology emphasizes faith lived through action, particularly in solidarity with the poor and oppressed.15 Religious life at Attica frequently reflected this form of engaged faith. Spiritual belief was not separate from prisoners’ material conditions but closely connected to their struggle for dignity and justice.

Central to this theological framework is the belief that human dignity is inherent and cannot be erased by social institutions. Liberation theology also emphasizes distributive justice, arguing that social institutions must meet basic human needs and prioritize the well-being of society’s most vulnerable members.15

From this perspective, conditions at Attica — overcrowding, racial discrimination, inadequate food, and restrictions on religious practice — represented not simply administrative failures but moral violations of human dignity.

When prisoners declared during the uprising that “we are men,” they were asserting precisely this claim: that imprisonment did not erase their humanity.

Accounts from the uprising also describe prisoners taking vestments from the prison chapel and wearing them during the occupation of D Yard.16 The act symbolically inverted the relationship between religious authority and institutional power, reclaiming religious imagery in support of prisoners’ own claims to dignity.

The Theological Stakes of State Violence

The state assault on September 13 carried theological as well as political significance. The willingness of the state to use lethal force against both prisoners and hostages in order to reassert control raised fundamental moral questions about the nature of punishment and justice.

For many observers, the violence at Attica reinforced prisoners’ arguments that the prison system functioned less as a rehabilitative institution than as a mechanism of control. Liberation theology often describes such systems as forms of structural sin — injustice embedded within institutions rather than located solely in individual actions.15

Within this framework, the violence at Attica revealed deeper tensions between state authority and human dignity. If punishment requires the sacrifice of human life to preserve institutional order, it raises questions about whether the system itself can be morally justified.

Religion and the Limits of Reform

In the years following Attica, prison systems across the United States gradually expanded religious programming and recognized Muslim prisoners’ rights to practice their faith. Chaplaincy programs grew, and courts increasingly affirmed incarcerated people’s religious rights.

Yet the deeper questions raised by Attica remain unresolved. Liberation theology calls not only for charity but for justice — transforming the social structures that produce suffering in the first place.15

Whether prisons, as institutions built on confinement and control, can fully meet that challenge remains an open question.
