Biography & Chronology

Key moments in the uprising and the intellectual production that confinement shaped.

Context: Conditions at Attica

In September 1971, Attica Correctional Facility held over 2,200 people in a facility built for approximately 1,600. The incarcerated population was majority Black and Puerto Rican, while the officer corps was virtually all-white and the prison administration entirely white. These demographic mismatches were not incidental; they reflected the broader racial geography of mass incarceration in the United States, where policing, sentencing, and confinement disproportionately targeted communities of color.

Conditions included severe overcrowding, inadequate medical care, limited access to educational programming, censorship of mail, restrictions on religious practice, and a pervasive culture of dehumanization. Prisoners received one shower per week, one roll of toilet paper per month, and were subjected to routine physical abuse. These material deprivations were not mere administrative failures—they were the architecture of a system designed to strip incarcerated people of dignity and agency.

The Attica Liberation Faction

During the summer of 1971, incarcerated people at Attica formed the "Attica Liberation Faction," a collective that engaged in political education, study groups, and the articulation of formal demands. Drawing on the broader currents of the Black Power movement, anti-colonial struggles, and prisoners' rights organizing at other institutions, the Faction drafted a list of demands and presented them to corrections leadership before the September uprising.

This organizing was not spontaneous. It emerged from years of intellectual labor inside the prison—reading, discussion, debate, and the development of a shared political analysis. The Faction's work exemplifies how confinement can become a site of knowledge production: people who were meant to be silenced instead developed a sophisticated critique of the systems that confined them.

"We are men! We are not beasts and do not intend to be beaten or driven as such." — The Attica Liberation Faction, July 1971

Timeline of Events

Summer 1971

Organizing and Demands

The Attica Liberation Faction forms. Prisoners organize study groups, develop political education programs, and draft a formal list of demands addressing conditions, rights, and dignity. A hunger strike follows the killing of George Jackson at San Quentin on August 21.

September 9, 1971

The Uprising Begins

Prisoners seize control of key areas of the facility, including D Yard. Officer William Quinn is fatally injured during the initial takeover. Prisoners take approximately 40 staff members hostage and begin organizing democratic governance of the occupied space.

September 10, 1971

Representatives and Observers

Prisoners elect representatives to negotiate on their behalf. Citizen observers—including journalists, lawyers, and elected officials—are permitted into D Yard to witness conditions and facilitate negotiations with Commissioner Russell G. Oswald.

September 11, 1971

The Manifesto

Prisoner representatives present a manifesto containing 28 demands. Commissioner Oswald accepts most of them, but refuses amnesty for participants. Officer Quinn dies of his injuries, making amnesty an even more politically charged issue. The demand for amnesty becomes the central impasse.

September 12, 1971

Rockefeller Refuses

Governor Nelson Rockefeller refuses requests to visit Attica personally. Whether his appearance could have de-escalated the crisis remains one of the central historiographical disputes. The state resolves to retake the facility by force if hostages are not released.

September 13, 1971

The Assault

After an ultimatum, state police and corrections officers launch an armed retaking. Tear gas is dropped from helicopters, followed by a barrage of nearly 2,000 rounds. In the assault, 29 incarcerated individuals and 10 hostages are killed. Many more are wounded. The shooting is later described as "near-indiscriminate."

September 14, 1971

The Lie Exposed

Monroe County pathologist Dr. John Edland publicly refutes official claims that prisoners had fatally slit hostages' throats. Autopsies confirm that all hostages killed during the retaking died from law-enforcement gunfire. The misinformation had already been broadcast nationally, conditioning public perception and justifying retaliatory violence against surrendered prisoners.

The Dead

Forty-three people died as a result of the Attica uprising and its retaking: 11 employees and 32 incarcerated individuals. Of these, 39 were killed during the state assault on September 13 or died of injuries sustained that day. The names of the dead are not incidental details. They represent the human cost of a system that produced the conditions for crisis and then responded with lethal force.

Hostages Killed by Prisoners

The last statistic bears emphasis: despite initial state claims, no hostage was fatally harmed by prisoners during the retaking. Every hostage who died on September 13 was killed by law-enforcement gunfire. This fact, confirmed by medical examination and later official investigations, fundamentally reframes the narrative of the assault from a "rescue operation" to an act of state violence.

Aftermath: Legal and Political

The legal aftermath of Attica revealed a striking asymmetry in accountability. The state pursued 42 indictments covering 62 incarcerated individuals on 1,289 counts. By contrast, only one state trooper was indicted—for reckless endangerment—and that indictment was later dropped along with all remaining charges.

Special Deputy Attorney General Alfred J. Scotti attributed the inability to prosecute law-enforcement officers for excessive force to "flagrant deficiencies" in evidence collection by the New York State Police—the same agency that carried out the assault. The Meyer Report, a three-volume investigation completed in 1975, documented these failures in detail, but its most critical volumes were sealed until 2015.

Civil litigation continued for decades. In January 2000, a $12 million settlement provided $8 million to former inmates and families and $4 million in legal fees. In January 2005, a separate $12 million settlement compensated surviving hostage employees and the estates of those killed. In both cases, no wrongdoing was formally admitted.

"The state's violence did not end when the shooting stopped. It continued in the cover-up, in the asymmetry of prosecution, and in the decades-long refusal to acknowledge what happened." — Analytical reflection
