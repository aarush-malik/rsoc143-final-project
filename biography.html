Biography & Chronology

Key moments in the uprising and the intellectual production that confinement shaped.

Context: Conditions at Attica

In September 1971, Attica Correctional Facility held over 2,200 people in a facility built for approximately 1,200. The incarcerated population was majority Black and Puerto Rican, while the officer corps was virtually all-white and the prison administration entirely white. These demographic mismatches reflected the broader racial geography of mass incarceration in the United States, where policing, sentencing, and confinement disproportionately targeted communities of color.

Daily life at Attica was defined by scarcity and control. Prisoners received one shower per week and one roll of toilet paper per month. Medical care was limited, educational programming was scarce, and mail was heavily censored. Religious practice faced restrictions, and reports of routine physical abuse by guards were common.

These conditions were not simply administrative failures. For many incarcerated people, they represented the deliberate structure of a system that stripped them of dignity, autonomy, and basic human rights.

The Attica Liberation Faction

By the summer of 1971, many prisoners at Attica had begun organizing politically. They formed the Attica Liberation Faction, a collective dedicated to political education and coordinated action inside the prison.

Members held study groups and discussions, reading widely and debating the meaning of incarceration, race, and freedom. Their thinking drew from the broader political movements of the time, including the Black Power movement, anti-colonial struggles abroad, and the growing prisoners’ rights movement. Writings by incarcerated intellectuals such as George Jackson, whose book Soledad Brother circulated widely among prisoners, were part of these conversations.

Jackson’s death at San Quentin on August 21, 1971, just weeks before the uprising, reverberated through prisons across the country. At Attica, prisoners organized a hunger strike in solidarity, further intensifying political discussion and collective organizing inside the facility.

From these conversations emerged a formal list of demands addressing prison conditions, civil rights, and political recognition, which prisoners presented to Attica officials shortly before the uprising.

"We have set forth demands that will bring us closer to the reality of the demise of these prison institutions that serve no useful purpose to the people of America, but to those who would enslave and exploit the people of America." — The Attica Liberation Faction, September 1971

Timeline of Events

Summer 1971

Organizing and Demands

Political organizing intensifies within the prison. Members of the Attica Liberation Faction develop study groups and draft a list of demands calling for improved conditions and greater rights. A hunger strike follows the killing of Black activist George Jackson at San Quentin on August 21.

September 9, 1971

The Uprising Begins

Prisoners seize control of parts of the facility, including D Yard, during a chaotic confrontation with guards. Officer William Quinn is fatally injured. Approximately forty prison employees are taken hostage as prisoners establish control of the yard and begin organizing internally.

September 10, 1971

Representatives and Observers

Prisoners elect representatives to negotiate with the state. A group of citizen observers—including journalists, lawyers, and elected officials—is allowed into D Yard to witness conditions and help facilitate negotiations with Commissioner Russell G. Oswald.

September 11, 1971

The Manifesto

Prison representatives present a manifesto containing 28 demands. Commissioner Oswald agrees to many of the demands but refuses to grant amnesty to participants in the uprising. Officer Quinn dies from his injuries, making the question of amnesty even more politically contentious.

September 12, 1971

Rockefeller Refuses

Governor Nelson Rockefeller (R-NY) declines repeated requests to visit Attica in person. Historians continue to debate whether his presence might have helped de-escalate the crisis. The state begins preparing to retake the prison by force.

September 13, 1971

The Assault

After an ultimatum, state police and corrections officers launch an armed retaking. Tear gas is dropped from helicopters, followed by a barrage of nearly 2,000 rounds. In the assault, 29 incarcerated individuals and 10 hostages are killed. Many more are wounded. The shooting is later described as "near-indiscriminate."

September 14, 1971

The Lie Exposed

Monroe County pathologist Dr. John Edland publicly refutes official claims that prisoners had fatally slit hostages' throats. Autopsies confirm that all hostages killed during the retaking died from law-enforcement gunfire. The misinformation had already been broadcast nationally, conditioning public perception and justifying retaliatory violence against surrendered prisoners.

The 28 Demands

On September 11, prisoners presented a formal manifesto to Commissioner Russell Oswald. The demands ranged from immediate material conditions — food, medical care, sanitation — to structural claims about labor rights, legal representation, and racial equity. Oswald accepted most. The sticking point was amnesty.

  1. Constitutional rights of legal representation at the time of all parole board hearings.
  2. Change in medical staff and medical policy. The Attica Prison hospital is totally inadequate, understaffed, and prejudiced in the treatment of inmates. Sufficient licensed practitioners available 24 hours a day.
  3. Adequate visiting conditions and facilities for inmates and their families.
  4. End to the segregation of prisoners from the general population because of their political beliefs.
  5. End to the persecution and punishment of prisoners who exercise the constitutional right of peaceful dissent, including the right to refuse work when not in violation of law.
  6. End to political and racial persecution, and the denial of prisoners' rights to subscribe to political papers, books, or any other educational and current media forwarded through the United States mail.
  7. Allow outside industries to enter the institutions and employ inmates to work eight-hour days at standard wage scales.
  8. Grant inmates the right to join or form labor unions.
  9. Allow inmates to support their own families through earned wages.
  10. Prosecute correctional officers as a matter of law for any act of cruel and unusual punishment where it is not a matter of life or death.
  11. Conform all inmate labor to state and federal minimum wage laws.
  12. End the escalating practice of physical brutality being perpetrated upon the inmates of New York State prisons.
  13. Provide three lawyers, at state expense, as a legal aid and liaison between inmates and the administration.
  14. Update industry working conditions to conform with state and federal codes covering factories.
  15. Establish worker's compensation for injuries sustained in the course of work.
  16. Create unionized vocational training programs comparable to those available through the federal prison system.
  17. Provide an annual accounting of the Recreation Fund and allow an inmate committee to have input into its expenditure.
  18. Replace the governor-appointed parole board with a popularly elected board reflecting the composition of the incarcerated population.
  19. Create a full-time salaried board of overseers to evaluate inmate complaints and review administrative actions.
  20. End administrative agitation of racial tensions among the inmate population.
  21. Provide ethnic counselors and programs for Black and Hispanic prisoner populations.
  22. End discrimination in parole judgments; end racial quotas in release decisions affecting Black and Brown prisoners.
  23. Allow inmates to be present during all searches of their cells and personal property.
  24. End parole discrimination based on prior records; limit all life sentences to a maximum of ten years.
  25. Improve the quality of food; provide water at meal tables; accommodate religious and medical dietary requirements.
  26. End unsanitary conditions in the mess hall and food preparation areas.
  27. Establish uniform rules and conditions across all New York State correctional facilities.
  28. Grant complete amnesty — freedom from any physical, mental, and legal reprisals — for all actions taken during the uprising.

The Dead

43 people died as a result of the Attica uprising and its retaking: 32 incarcerated individuals and 11 employees. Of these, 39 were killed during the state assault on September 13 or died of injuries sustained that day.

Hostages Killed by Prisoners

The last statistic bears emphasis: despite initial state claims, no hostage was fatally harmed by prisoners during the retaking. Every hostage who died on September 13 was killed by law-enforcement gunfire. This fact, confirmed by medical examination and later official investigations, fundamentally reframes the narrative of the assault from a "rescue operation" to an act of state violence.

Aftermath: Legal, Political, and Moral Reckoning

The legal aftermath of Attica revealed a striking asymmetry in accountability. Prosecutors pursued 42 indictments against 62 incarcerated individuals, charging them with 1,289 criminal counts related to the uprising. By contrast, only one state trooper was indicted, on a charge of reckless endangerment. That charge was later dismissed along with the remaining prosecutions.

Special Deputy Attorney General Alfred J. Scotti later attributed the failure to prosecute law-enforcement officers for excessive force to what he called "flagrant deficiencies" in evidence collection by the New York State Police—the same agency responsible for carrying out the assault. The Meyer Report, a three-volume investigation completed in 1975, documented these failures in detail. Its most critical volumes, however, were sealed for decades and not fully released until 2015, long after many of the people involved had died.

Civil litigation continued for decades. In January 2000, New York State reached a $12 million settlement with former prisoners and their families, allocating $8 million in damages and $4 million in legal fees. In January 2005, the state agreed to a second $12 million settlement compensating surviving hostage employees and the families of those killed during the assault. In both cases, the state provided financial compensation while formally denying wrongdoing.

For many observers—including clergy, theologians, and religious activists—the aftermath of Attica raised deeper moral questions about justice itself. Religious traditions often frame justice not only as punishment for wrongdoing but as truth-telling, accountability, and the restoration of dignity to those harmed. From this perspective, the decades-long struggle for recognition after Attica reflected a search for moral acknowledgment in the absence of legal accountability.

The prisoners at Attica had framed their demands in explicitly moral language. When they declared during the uprising that "we are men", they were asserting a claim about human dignity as much as a political demand for better conditions. The legal aftermath of Attica left that claim unresolved. Courts produced settlements and reports, but the deeper questions raised by the uprising—about justice, punishment, and the value of human life within the prison system—remain open.

"The state's violence did not end when the shooting stopped. It continued in the cover-up, in the asymmetry of prosecution, and in the decades-long refusal to acknowledge what happened." — Analytical reflection
