The Attica Uprising of 1971

Knowledge, Resistance, and the Carceral State

What kinds of knowledge emerge from confinement, and what does incarceration reveal about freedom, justice, and disappearance?

Project Overview

On September 9, 1971, incarcerated people at Attica Correctional Facility in western New York seized control of the prison. What followed was a four-day standoff, a set of articulate political demands, and a state retaking that killed 43 people and exposed the deep contradictions at the heart of American punishment. The Attica uprising was not a riot born of chaos. It was a political action rooted in years of organizing, study, and collective critique of the carceral state.

This project centers the incarcerated people of Attica not as objects of state violence alone, but as producers of knowledge—theological, political, cultural, and ethical knowledge that emerged from conditions of confinement, exclusion, and disappearance. Their manifesto, their demands, their organizing structures, and the very language they used to articulate freedom constitute an intellectual tradition that the carceral state attempted to silence.

The Central Question

What kinds of knowledge emerge from confinement, and what does incarceration reveal about freedom, justice, and disappearance?

This project takes seriously the intellectual labor of those whom the carceral state attempts to disappear. The task is not simply to document their lives, but to listen to their ideas, critiques, and visions of freedom. The people of Attica produced manifestos, organized democratic structures, articulated legal and moral demands, and—in the face of state violence—demonstrated that confinement could not confine thought.

Through the lenses of religion, race, nation, class, and the prison itself, this site examines how incarceration functions as a site of knowledge production rather than only social exclusion, and how the voices silenced by the state continue to speak to questions of justice and freedom.
