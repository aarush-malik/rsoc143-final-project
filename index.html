The Attica Riot Uprising

Resistance, Religion, and the Carceral State

What kinds of knowledge emerge from confinement, and what does incarceration reveal about freedom, justice, and disappearance?

Project Overview

On September 9, 1971, over a thousand incarcerated people seized control of Attica Correctional Facility in western New York. The next four days brought negotiations, a set of demands that read as a political manifesto, and a state assault that killed 43 people. The official story called it a riot. It wasn’t.

The Attica uprising was the product of years of organizing, study, and collective political consciousness. The people of Attica had built democratic structures, studied law, read theology, and developed a coherent critique of American punishment. When the state retook the prison by force, it was more than suppressing a rebellion; it was them trying to silence a body of ideas.

This project centers the incarcerated people of Attica not only as victims of state violence, but as thinkers and organizers whose ideas emerged from life inside the prison. In their manifesto, political demands, and collective organizing, they developed powerful critiques of punishment, race, and freedom. Even within a system designed to silence them, the people of Attica produced ideas that continue to challenge how we understand justice.

The Central Question

What kinds of knowledge emerge from confinement, and what does incarceration reveal about freedom, justice, and disappearance?

This project takes seriously the intellectual labor of those whom the carceral state attempts to disappear. The task is not simply to document their lives, but to listen to their ideas, critiques, and visions of freedom. The people of Attica produced manifestos, organized democratic structures, articulated legal and moral demands, and—in the face of state violence—demonstrated that confinement could not confine thought.

Through the lenses of religion, race, nation, class, and the prison itself, this site examines how incarceration functions as a site of knowledge production rather than only social exclusion, and how the voices silenced by the state continue to speak to questions of justice and freedom.
