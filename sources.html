Sources & Evidence

Primary documents, official records, and scholarly works that inform this project.

Source Priority Framework

This project organizes its evidence base into three tiers, reflecting proximity to the events, institutional authority, and the capacity for independent verification.

1. Official Records

Contemporaneous official records and later-released official investigations grounded in subpoena power, grand-jury processes, autopsy authority, or court adjudication. These sources are strongest for establishing facts about casualties, legal proceedings, and institutional actions.

2. Contemporary Coverage

Contemporaneous national political and news coverage, valuable for establishing "what was said and when," particularly around misinformation dynamics and political framing of the crisis.

3. Scholarship

Peer-reviewed or professionally curated scholarship and retrospectives, valuable for interpretation, triangulation, and theoretical framing. Weaker than Tier One for counts and causation unless explicitly drawing on primary data.

Key Investigations

The McKay Commission (1971–1972)

Appointed by Governor Rockefeller on November 15, 1971, the New York State Special Commission on Attica was chaired by Robert B. McKay (Dean of NYU Law School) and empowered under New York's Moreland Act with subpoena authority. The Commission's published report (September 13, 1972) was "highly critical" of Rockefeller, the State Police, and corrections officials for poor planning and the rapid resort to lethal force. It specifically concluded that Rockefeller "should not have committed" armed forces without appearing at the prison and determining that no alternative existed.

The Meyer Report (1975)

After prosecutor Malcolm Bell alleged in May 1975 that his superiors were covering up officer crimes, Governor Hugh Carey appointed Judge Bernard S. Meyer to investigate. The resulting three-volume report, delivered in October 1975, documented systematic evidentiary failures and "flagrant deficiencies" in evidence collection by state police after the retaking. Volume 1 was made public in 1975; Volumes 2 and 3 were sealed in 1981 and released only in redacted form in May 2015.

The Attica Task Force(s)

"Attica Task Force" refers to more than one entity. The original 1970s Task Force was formed by Special Deputy Attorney General Robert Fischer to oversee criminal investigation and prosecution. A separate 2001 Task Force was connected to issues raised by the "Forgotten Victims of Attica" (hostages and survivors) and addressed hostage-family concerns.

Casualty Verification

The total death count of 43 is consistently reported across official sources (New York State Archives, DOCCS). These deaths break down as follows:

Category Count Context
Incarcerated people killed in September 13 retaking 29 Killed by law-enforcement gunfire during the armed assault
Hostage employees killed in September 13 retaking 10 All killed by law-enforcement gunfire, not by prisoners
Incarcerated people killed during standoff 3 Killed by other prisoners during the initial takeover period
Employee killed during initial takeover 1 Officer William Quinn, fatally injured September 9, died September 11
Total 43

Injury counts are less consistently reported. The Marshall Project gives 85 prisoners wounded, 3 hostages wounded, and 1 state trooper wounded in the September 13 assault. Prisoner testimony claims approximately 300 wounded, a figure that may include beatings and injuries beyond the D Yard assault.

Evidentiary Gaps and Contested Facts

Several important questions remain incompletely resolved in the available evidentiary record:

  • Hostage counts: The number of people held hostage varies across sources (38 in the Nixon transcript footnote vs. higher counts in other retellings), reflecting different definitions and timeframes.
  • Injury counts: The absence of a single consolidated medical ledger in open sources means precise injury figures remain contested.
  • Misinformation chain: The full causal chain between the false throat-slitting narrative, officer behavior, and post-retaking abuse cannot be fully documented without complete contemporaneous personnel records.
  • Meyer Report completeness: Redactions in the 2015 release of Volumes 2–3 leave room for conflicting narratives about command responsibility and cover-up.
  • Incarcerated casualty identities: Official state sources listing all incarcerated decedents by name are not readily accessible; memorial rosters fill the gap but are not authoritative government records.
