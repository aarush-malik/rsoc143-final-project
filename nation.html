Nation

How did the people of Attica understand the nation-state—as just, unjust, redeemable, or fundamentally broken?

The Nation-State on Trial

The Attica uprising placed the American nation-state itself on trial. The demands articulated by incarcerated people were not simply requests for better conditions within the existing system. They were, implicitly and often explicitly, indictments of a national project that claimed to guarantee liberty and justice while operating the largest system of human confinement in the world. For the people inside Attica, the contradiction between American democratic ideals and American carceral practice was not an abstract philosophical problem. It was the condition of their daily existence.

Sovereignty and the Yard

During the four days of the standoff, D Yard functioned as a kind of counter-sovereign space. Prisoners elected representatives, organized security, distributed food and medical care, and developed a governance structure that was, in many respects, more democratic than the institution that surrounded it. This improvised political community was a practical demonstration of an alternative vision of governance—one rooted in collective self-determination rather than coercive control.

The state's response to this experiment in self-governance was revealing. Rather than engaging with the political substance of the prisoners' demands, the state treated the occupation of D Yard as an intolerable challenge to its monopoly on legitimate violence. Governor Rockefeller's refusal to appear at the prison and his decision to authorize an armed retaking reflected a political calculus in which the reassertion of state sovereignty took precedence over the lives of both prisoners and hostages.

Nixon, Rockefeller, and the "Ransom" Frame

President Nixon's response to Attica, delivered in a September 16, 1971 news conference, is revealing for its framing. Nixon described the crisis as evidence of "the problem of prisons in the United States" while simultaneously defending Rockefeller's use of force and warning against "giving in to demands for ransom." The word "ransom" is significant: it recasts political demands as criminal extortion, transforming the prisoners from political actors articulating grievances into hostage-takers making illegitimate threats.

This rhetorical move exemplifies how the nation-state delegitimizes challenges from below. By refusing to recognize the political content of the prisoners' demands, Nixon and Rockefeller could frame the assault as a law-enforcement operation rather than a political suppression. The language of "ransom" and "rescue" obscured the reality that the state's own violence killed every hostage who died on September 13.

"We must not give in to demands for ransom." — President Richard Nixon, September 16, 1971

The framing of legitimate political demands as "ransom" reveals the nation-state's refusal to recognize incarcerated people as political subjects capable of making valid claims on the state.

Anti-Colonial Analysis

Many of the incarcerated organizers at Attica drew explicitly on anti-colonial thought. Influenced by Frantz Fanon, Malcolm X, and the broader Third World liberation movements of the era, they understood the American prison as a colonial institution—one that confined colonized populations within the borders of the colonizing nation itself. This analysis reframed incarceration as an extension of the same logic that governed imperial domination abroad: the subjugation of racialized populations through violence, confinement, and the extraction of labor.

The anti-colonial framework allowed prisoners to connect their experience to global struggles for liberation and to understand the American nation-state not as a neutral arbiter of justice but as an active agent of racial and imperial violence. From this perspective, the prison was not a failure of the nation but one of its most essential institutions—the mechanism through which the state managed the populations it had created through slavery, segregation, and economic exclusion.

The Question of Redemption

Did the people of Attica believe the American nation-state was redeemable? The evidence suggests a range of positions. Some prisoners operated within a reformist framework, demanding constitutional rights, due process, and the enforcement of existing laws against cruel and unusual punishment. Their demands implicitly affirmed the legitimacy of the national legal order while insisting that it live up to its own promises.

Others articulated a more radical critique, viewing the nation-state as fundamentally structured by racial violence and therefore unreformable in any meaningful sense. For these thinkers, the prison was not a deviation from American democracy but its truest expression—the place where the state's commitments to hierarchy, exclusion, and coercive control were most clearly visible. The 28 demands can be read as straddling both positions: pragmatic enough to engage with existing institutions, radical enough to challenge the legitimacy of the entire system.

"The nation promised liberty. The prison revealed what that promise was worth." — Analytical reflection
