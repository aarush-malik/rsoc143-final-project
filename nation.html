Nation

How did the people of Attica understand the nation-state—as just, unjust, redeemable, or fundamentally broken?

Democracy and Freedom

The Attica uprising placed the American nation-state itself on trial and challenged the idea of democracy and freedom that the nation parades. The demands articulated by incarcerated people were not simply requests for better conditions within the prison, but an explicit critique on the values the nation claims to uphold. Within the Attica Liberation Faction Manifesto of Demands that the rioters wrote, the incarcerated people detail the violation and victimization of their persons.

They articulate how they are the embodiment of “...the denial of the celebrated due process of law.” They further describe that they have been “...murdered, brutalized and framed on various criminal charges because [they] seek the rights and privileges of all American people.”34 This call for action is not merely targeted towards the Attica prison, but the entirety of the nation and its carceral system.

Nixon and Rockefeller

Though Governor Nelson Rockefeller refused to personally negotiate with the demands that the imprisoned people were making, he made the decision to launch an armed assault against the rebellion.35 This assault resulted in the killing of 29 incarcerated people and 10 correctional officers and employees.36 President Nixon believed that the armed response from law enforcement would send a message to activists.37

Clearly, the suppression of the rebellion had greater implications for the federal government as they believed that this singular Attica rebellion was a national threat that could spark other uprisings. They worked to conceal what happened in Attica to prevent it from inspiring other activists across the nation both in and out of prisons. By doing so, there is an implicit acknowledgement of the role of prisons within American society and politics.37

When the incarcerated people in Attica decided to stand against their oppression, they contributed to a larger social movement against the American government and the hierarchies they uphold.

Furthermore, Nixon claimed that public officials could not “give in to demands for ransom.”38 The rhetoric in this statement exemplifies the ways in which the pleas of imprisoned people to be treated as humans and with dignity become diluted and misconstrued as illegitimate and criminal.

By refusing to appropriately recognize the demands of the incarcerated people, Nixon and Rockefeller were able to obscure the violence that the state inflicted onto the imprisoned people and reframe it as a rescue mission to retrieve those who were being held hostage. Additionally, Nixon’s rhetoric purposely avoids recognizing incarcerated people as worthy of making valid claims against the state.

The Question of Redemption

The people within Attica prison questioned the authority and rightfulness of the American nation-state. In their manifesto, they made several demands pointing to the constitutionality and validity of the conditions to which they were subjected to.

While the rebellion adopted an incredibly reformist framework that affirmed the legitimacy of the national legal order, they called for the nation to live up to the promises and rights they claim to guarantee to all American people. In other words, the manifesto alludes to how following adequate policy and procedure would foster better conditions for incarcerated people while not necessarily calling for the abolition of the prison and the carceral system.

These demands, while not abolitionist, are a major stepping stone towards the destruction of the carceral system.
