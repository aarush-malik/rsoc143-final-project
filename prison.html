The Prison

How did the people of Attica understand the prison itself—as a theological space, a site of social death, a tool of racial control?

The Social Death Structure of Attica

Attica Correctional Facility was a maximum-security prison built in rural Attica, New York. Its steel and concrete walls were designed to prevent escape, and their surfaces were intentionally made slippery so they could not be scaled. The facility contained four separate cellblocks, and the prison yards were divided in order to discourage collective action or large gatherings among prisoners. The central point of the complex, known as “Times Square,” functioned as the main corridor connecting the prison’s internal spaces. Tear gas devices were even built directly into the walls as a security measure.

On paper, Attica appeared almost self-contained. The prison included what administrators described as a full institutional infrastructure: a farm, hospital, textile factory, tailor shop, school, auditorium, laundry facility, and its own power plant. Officials also pointed to features such as mattresses, recreation rooms, and access to sunlight in cells as evidence that the institution provided adequate living conditions.

For those incarcerated there, however, daily life looked very different. By 1971, more than 2,200 people were confined in a facility designed for roughly 1,600. Prisoners were locked in their cells for between fourteen and sixteen hours each day and worked prison jobs for wages that averaged only a few cents per hour. Showers were permitted only once a week. Former prisoner Frank “Big Black” Smith later described the experience bluntly: “a shower to us in Attica state prison is a bucket of water.”

Conditions like these produced what scholars often describe as a form of social death. Prisoners were not only physically confined but stripped of meaningful autonomy, separated from their communities, and subjected to constant surveillance and control.

Prison as Racial Control

The conditions at Attica were closely tied to the racial politics of the late 1960s and early 1970s. During this period of intense social unrest, prisons increasingly became what some observers described as a “dumping ground” for urban Black and Hispanic men facing drug charges.

Federal policies helped accelerate this trend. President Richard Nixon’s declaration of a national “War on Drugs” expanded law enforcement powers and dramatically increased the number of arrests in Black and Brown communities. Additional measures, including the Law Enforcement Assistance Act and the creation of mandatory minimum sentencing policies, strengthened the criminal justice system’s ability to incarcerate large numbers of people for drug-related offenses.

By 1970, the racial structure of Attica reflected these developments. Approximately 85 percent of the prison population was Black or Puerto Rican, while nearly all correctional officers and administrators were white. Roughly three quarters of the incarcerated population had not completed high school. These disparities made the prison an intensely unequal social environment.

Attica’s Prisoners and the Institution

Former prisoner Arthur Harrison later summarized the experience of incarceration in stark terms: “They treated us like we weren’t human.” His description reflects not only individual abuse but the broader structure of the institution itself.

Outside prison walls, the civil rights movement was reshaping American society. By the 1960s, many of these political conversations had begun to reach incarcerated communities as well. Earlier prison protests, including the uprising at Auburn Correctional Facility, demonstrated how quickly demands for dignity and recognition could escalate when authorities refused to acknowledge them.

Political organizations such as the Black Panther Party gained influence during this period, advocating resistance to government exploitation and racial injustice. At the same time, federal agencies including the FBI worked aggressively to dismantle these movements, leading to the arrest and imprisonment of many activists. Within this broader political climate, prisons like Attica became sites where political repression and racial control intersected.

The killing of Black Panther activist George Jackson at San Quentin in August 1971 intensified these tensions across the American prison system. News of his death spread rapidly among incarcerated people and helped galvanize political organizing inside Attica.

Survivors also described routine violence inside the facility. Arthur Harrison and filmmaker Stanley Nelson both recalled that guards often formed informal “goon squads” that would enter cells at night and beat prisoners with little accountability. In this environment, the uprising that followed was not a sudden explosion but the culmination of years of frustration, political awareness, and institutional abuse.

Attica in Popular Culture

The Attica uprising has continued to shape public memory through documentaries, journalism, and film. The 2021 documentary Attica, directed by Stanley Nelson and Traci Curry, draws on archival footage and interviews with survivors to reconstruct the events of the uprising and its aftermath.

Representations of Attica also appear in television and film, reflecting the lasting cultural significance of the uprising and its role in shaping debates about incarceration in the United States.
