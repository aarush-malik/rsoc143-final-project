The Prison

How did the people of Attica understand the prison itself—as a theological space, a site of social death, a tool of racial control?

The Prison and the Economy

The incarcerated people of Attica understood their confinement as inseparable from their economic position. The overwhelming majority was made up of men of color who came from poor urban communities that had been systematically excluded from economic opportunity through housing discrimination, labor market segmentation, educational disinvestment, and the targeted policing of poverty.1 The prison therefore functioned as part of a broader system that absorbs those who are poor and working class and removes them from public life.2

Not only was the prison population largely composed of working-class men of color, but the prison itself functioned as an economic institution that generated revenue and employment for the state. For example, California prisons were allocated $13.6 billion in the 2025–26 state budget, funding guards, transportation, food services, infrastructure, and other systems that sustain local economies.3 While Attica existed in a different historical moment, prisons have long functioned as economic engines for the communities that host them.

Inside Attica, prisoners also engaged in political education that helped them analyze these dynamics. A group of incarcerated men spent the summer before the uprising reading revolutionary texts by Karl Marx, Vladimir Lenin, Leon Trotsky, Malcolm X, W.E.B. Du Bois, and Frederick Douglass.4 These readings encouraged discussion of how class, race, and economic power shaped the conditions prisoners experienced.

Prison Labor and Exploitation

One of the most concrete economic grievances at Attica concerned prison labor. Incarcerated people were required to work in prison industries, laundry facilities, kitchens, and maintenance operations for wages so low that many described them as exploitative. At the time of the uprising, most prisoners earned only 6–29 cents a day for their labor, while the prison generated roughly $1.2 million in sales from the goods produced inside.5

This labor created economic value for the state and for outside contractors while returning almost nothing to the workers themselves or to their families. Prisoners' demands therefore addressed labor directly, calling for fair wages, minimum wage protections, and an end to the exploitation of incarcerated workers.

Many prisoners also connected prison labor to the longer history of coerced labor in the United States. The Thirteenth Amendment formally abolished slavery and involuntary servitude “except as a punishment for crime.”6 After the amendment’s passage, systems such as convict leasing disproportionately targeted Black men, effectively reproducing forced labor through the criminal legal system. Prisoners at Attica understood their labor conditions within this broader historical framework.

Class, Community, and Confinement

The social position of prisoners extended beyond the prison walls. Many incarcerated people recognized that the communities they came from were themselves heavily surveilled and economically marginalized. Neighborhoods characterized by concentrated poverty, aggressive policing, and the withdrawal of public services often functioned as entry points into the criminal legal system.

In this sense, the prison represented the end point of a pipeline that began long before incarceration. The U.S. prison population expanded dramatically in the decades following Attica, increasing by more than 700 percent between the early 1970s and the early 2000s.7 Many scholars now describe this dynamic as a “ghetto-to-prison pipeline,” in which economic exclusion and racial segregation increase the likelihood of incarceration.

Michelle Alexander later described this system in The New Jim Crow, arguing that many communities are effectively “caged off” from educational and economic opportunity through structures of surveillance and punishment.8 The people of Attica did not necessarily use this academic language, but many had experienced both the conditions of marginalized urban neighborhoods and the realities of incarceration itself.

The Economics of the Retaking

The economic logic of the carceral system also shaped the state’s response to the uprising. Governor Nelson Rockefeller ultimately chose to retake the prison by force rather than continue negotiations with the prisoners.9 Granting the prisoners’ demands — including improved conditions, fair wages, and potential amnesty — would have required structural changes and ongoing expenditures.

In contrast, the violent retaking of the prison, though catastrophic in human cost, avoided these structural reforms. From this perspective, the assault represented not only a political decision but also an economic one.

The legal aftermath reinforced this pattern. In 2000, the state agreed to a $12 million settlement for former prisoners and their families. A separate $12 million settlement in 2005 compensated surviving hostages and their families.10 These payments acknowledged harm but did not require major institutional changes to the prison system itself.

Class Solidarity Across the Walls

The hostage employees killed during the retaking were themselves largely working-class people. Many corrections officers came from rural communities in western New York where the prison was one of the primary sources of stable employment.11

This created a tragic dynamic in which working-class white guards oversaw working-class Black and Brown prisoners, both groups tied to an institution that ultimately served broader political and economic interests.

Prisoners recognized this contradiction. Some of their demands focused on education, rehabilitation, and broader reforms that could reduce incarceration altogether. The uprising therefore exposed a deeper class tension within the carceral system itself: working people on both sides of the prison walls were caught within a structure designed and maintained by political leadership far removed from the conditions inside.

"The prison does not stand apart from the economy. It is where the economy places the people it has no use for." — Analytical reflection
